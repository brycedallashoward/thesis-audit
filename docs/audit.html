The entire sequence is 20 days, with no SMS, no feedback capture, no follow-up after day 20. Both products talk about benefits over months, but the lifecycle ends before the customer has had a chance to feel the full effect.

3

People open every email but rarely click

Both

Both brands have the same pattern. Campaigns average ~47% opens, 0.5% clicks. Thesis M1 onboarding: 81K recipients, 57% opens, 1.3% clicks. Both accounts are running at a quarter of the DTC benchmark.

0.2–0.8% Campaign click rates
2–3% DTC benchmark
6.8% Mindshift (proof case)

The Mindshift series (flow Y6nDb2) is the proof case. It sends education content about the 30-day habit reset to the same Thesis audience and gets 6.8% clicks. Same people, same inbox, 5x the result. That gap means there’s real performance to gain by reworking what’s in these emails.

On Stasis, condition-specific angles land well in campaigns: “3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” “Social Battery.” But none of that shows up in checkout or onboarding flows. The same team writes both, but the messaging isn’t consistent across lifecycle touchpoints.

Recommendation

  • Audit the highest-volume flows and campaigns on both brands for CTA clarity
  • Model on Mindshift: lead with education, make the click worth something
  • Get the Stasis campaign angles (“3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash”) into checkout, onboarding, and retention flows where they’re currently absent
  • 0.5% → 2% click rate is a 4x lift on every send across both brands

We don’t know why Stasis customers cancel

Stasis

Stasis grew in 8 of 11 months. Jan–Feb 2026 alone added ~7,000 net subscribers. The cancel rate hasn’t moved: ~2,900–3,200 per month regardless of how many new people sign up. Acquisition doubled from October to February. Cancels stayed flat.

We can’t see why any of them leave. ReCharge cancellation events land in Klaviyo with empty properties. No reason, no product context, nothing. That data is in ReCharge’s native reporting only. So the winback and save flows send the same generic message to everyone, whether they cancelled over price, side effects, or because they meant to hit pause.

Fix the data and you can fix the messaging. Price objection gets a downgrade offer. Product fit gets a SKU swap. “Didn’t feel a difference” gets content about onset timelines. And if the top cancel reason turns out to be “didn’t feel anything,” that tells you onboarding needs to set expectations earlier.

Recommendation

  • Get cancel reason data from ReCharge. Direct access or an export. This unlocks everything below.
  • Build reason-specific save flows: price → downgrade/pause, product fit → SKU swap, “didn’t work” → onset timeline content, accidental cancel → one-click reactivation
  • Winback by reason, not by calendar date. Someone who left over price needs a different email than someone who had side effects.
  • Feed the trends back into onboarding. If the top cancel reason is “didn’t feel anything,” set expectations earlier.

165K churned subscribers are still reading, getting nothing useful

Both

Between both brands, about 165,000 people who cancelled are still opening emails. They tried it, they left, and they’re still reading. That’s a huge audience getting nothing worth responding to.

~165K Churned subs still opening
48–57% Winback open rates
0.21–0.25% Winback conversion

Thesis winback converts 0.21% across 46K people. Stasis converts 0.25% across 118K. Opens are 48–57%. They read it. They just don’t do anything because the email says “we miss you, come back” and nothing else.

What if you asked them something instead? SMS at 30 days post-cancel: “Got questions or feedback? Reply to this message.” In email, let them react: “How you felt on Stasis vs. without it.” If they say they felt the benefit, that’s a warm lead. If not, stop sending winback and point them somewhere else.

Recommendation

  • SMS at 30 days post-cancel. Ask a question, not for a purchase.
  • Interactive email modules that let the customer self-sort: “felt the benefit” vs. “didn’t notice a difference”
  • Branch on what they tell you. Warm candidates get a re-engagement path. Cold candidates stop getting winback.
  • Segment by what the customer says, not how long ago they cancelled.

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family: profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis. Messaging should feel like “we’re here for your whole family”, not separate product silos.

Combined portal: holistic digital experience across brands

Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family’s supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the “what to expect” content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM: Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks “How satisfied are you with your stimulants?”, gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There’s likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience: an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface.

Stimulant story discount: unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. Intentionally unstructured (free-text or voice) to capture authentic stories and language. Parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey.

Dynamic question series: progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails: a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization.

Community done right

Both Stasis and Thesis have modest community presences (under 3,000 followers/members each). Stasis emails are full of Instagram CTAs but the community itself hasn’t been built to support them. A concentrated community effort could turn this into a real pillar: peer support, product feedback, UGC for campaigns, and a retention lever that doesn’t depend on email or SMS.
