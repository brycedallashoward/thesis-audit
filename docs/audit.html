Stasis Kids is getting adult messaging

Stasis

Kids sold out in the test run, so demand isn’t the question. The question is what happens after they buy. Only 3 of 10 lifecycle touchpoints are Kids-specific. The other 7 send parents the same adult ADHD content, including post-purchase (no Kids flow live) and backorder (built but sitting in draft).

There is one working parent identification path: a Kids-specific pop-up that only shows on the Kids URL feeds the [Stasis Kids Email] [antidote] segment, and that segment is growing daily. So parents who land directly on the Kids page are being captured. But anyone who enters through the main quiz and selects “shopping for a child” gets no parent segmentation — that quiz response doesn’t sync to Klaviyo. The pop-up covers one entry point; the quiz gap misses everyone else.

  • Segment enforcement: parents should never receive adult ADHD creative, regardless of how they entered

Both

Both PDPs feature a “Commit to Consistency” timeline (Hours → Days → Weeks → Months). It shows up once in the post-purchase flow and then disappears. The entire sequence is 20 days: no SMS, no feedback capture, nothing after day 20. The product promises months of compounding benefits, but the lifecycle quits before the customer has a chance to feel them.

Stasis

The problem: we can’t see why any of them leave in Klaviyo. ReCharge cancel events land in Klaviyo with empty properties. No reason, no product context, nothing actionable. That data lives in ReCharge’s native reporting only, so the winback and save flows send the same generic message whether someone left over price, side effects, or because they meant to hit pause.

Fix the data pipe and the messaging fixes itself. Price objection gets a downgrade offer. Product fit gets a SKU swap. “Didn’t feel a difference” gets content about onset timelines. Recommendation

  • Build reason-specific save flows: price → downgrade/pause, product fit → SKU swap, “didn’t work” → onset timeline content, accidental cancel → one-click reactivation
  • Winback by reason, not by calendar date. Someone who left over price needs a different email than someone who had side effects.
  • Feed the trends back into onboarding. If the top cancel reason is “didn’t feel anything,” set expectations earlier.

  4. Segment by response, not by time — what the customer tells you matters more than how long ago they cancelled.

10

Only 5% of each brand’s marketable audience can receive SMS

Thesis: 49K SMS out of 973K profiles. Stasis: 12K SMS out of 205K profiles. Both ~5%. SMS converts best on both brands but barely anyone is on the list.
Both

Findings

  • SMS penetration: Thesis has 49,341 SMS subscribers out of 972,590 marketable profiles (5.1%). Stasis has 11,563 out of 205,000 (5.6%). The highest-converting channel on both brands reaches barely 1 in 20 customers.
  • Thesis SMS Welcome : best lead conversion channel ($6.23 RPR, 5.7% conv). But a $39 blast caused 5.15% unsub, list shrank 50.7K → 41.4K, now silent
  • Stasis SMS Welcome : $2.58 RPR, solid. But list is small (~12K) and abandonment flows are draft, not live
  • Neither brand uses SMS in lifecycle (onboarding, refill, winback)
  • Sign-up form gap: Thesis quiz popups (15.6% mobile submit rate) don’t include SMS capture. The highest-converting lead channel has no acquisition path beyond Welcome.
  • Opt-in friction: Both brands use rigorous double opt-in for SMS instead of smart opt-in. Neither collects SMS consent at checkout.

What higher SMS penetration is actually worth

The honest answer: we can’t model this as a linear revenue add. New SMS subscribers aren’t new customers; they’re existing email subscribers opting into a second channel. Some SMS-attributed purchases would have happened via email anyway. The real question is how revenue per customer changes when they’re reachable on both channels.

What we’d need to measure first:

Customer groupWhat to measureWhy it matters
Email-onlyAvg annual revenue per profileBaseline: what a single-channel customer is worth
SMS-onlyAvg annual revenue per profileRare group, but shows SMS-first conversion behavior
Email + SMSAvg annual revenue per profileThe lift: how much more a dual-channel customer spends

As SMS penetration grows from 5% to 15%, the customer base shifts from mostly email-only to a larger share of dual-channel subscribers. Revenue shifts with it, but it’s not additive — it’s a rate change on existing customers. The value of SMS isn’t “more revenue per message.” It’s reaching people at moments email can’t: feedback collection via reply, time-sensitive winback, cart abandonment within minutes. Those are conversations that don’t happen on email.

Recommendation

  • Rebuild Thesis SMS list with proper lifecycle usage, not blasts
  • Add SMS capture to Thesis sign-up forms
  • Switch both brands to smart opt-in and add SMS consent at checkout
  • Launch Stasis SMS abandonment flows (already built, sitting in draft)
  • Integrate SMS into onboarding, refill, and winback on both brands
  • Once SMS capture is fixed: build channel-aware flows that react to signup status and preferred channel. If someone is email+SMS, the cadence and content should differ from email-only. Campaigns should respect channel preference rather than blasting both.

Review collection is declining on Thesis; Stasis reviews are scattered across SKU iterations

Thesis: completion rate halved (1.4% → 0.7%). Stasis: the catalog has 50+ items across packaging generations. Reviews don’t carry over when a SKU changes, so counts reset with every product update.
Both

Findings

  • Thesis completion rate nearly halved over 12 months. Rating drifting 4.59 → 4.45. 5-star share dropped 82% → 72%.
  • Thesis negative themes: “doesn’t work,” GI issues, subscription friction, price, caffeine on Motivation, which map directly to churn reasons
  • Stasis SKU fragmentation: the Klaviyo product catalog has 50+ items. “Day & Night Set” alone exists as 6+ catalog entries across packaging iterations (plastic bottles → pouches → new packaging → welcome kits). Each new SKU starts reviews from zero. The 949 review count on Day+Night Set isn’t low collection, it’s reviews scattered across deprecated SKUs that no longer surface.
  • Kids products (added Aug 2025) are only 7 months old. Preorder restock SKUs and gummies variants add more fragmentation.
  • Unpublished test/legacy items in the catalog: Metabase Syncing Test, Eli Health Cortisol Test, TikTok Shop variants, earplugs, breathwork/meditation partnerships. These are experimentation artifacts, not product issues.

Recommendation

  • Thesis: mine negative review themes as a churn-reason proxy (useful until ReCharge cancel reasons from #4 are available)
  • Stasis: consolidate reviews across SKU iterations so current products inherit past reviews. Okendo supports review migration between product IDs.
  • Stasis: clean up the catalog. Archive unpublished test products and deprecated packaging variants that will never sell again.
  • Both: improve review request timing and incentive to lift completion rate

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family: profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis. Messaging should feel like “we’re here for your whole family”, not separate product silos.

Combined portal: holistic digital experience across brands

Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family’s supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the “what to expect” content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM: Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks “How satisfied are you with your stimulants?”, gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There’s likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience: an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface.

Stimulant story discount: unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. Intentionally unstructured (free-text or voice) to capture authentic stories and language. Parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey.

Dynamic question series: progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails: a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization.

Community done right

Both Stasis and Thesis have modest community presences (under 3,000 followers/members each). Stasis emails are full of Instagram CTAs but the community itself hasn’t been built to support them. A concentrated community effort could turn this into a real pillar: peer support, product feedback, UGC for campaigns, and a retention lever that doesn’t depend on email or SMS.
