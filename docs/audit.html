The account generates ~$2M/month and runs ~86 flows. On the surface it looks active and healthy. The real story is more concentrated: one retention flow accounts for the majority of attributed email revenue. The account's financial health depends almost entirely on that flow staying healthy and the subscriber base not shrinking further.

Since the Hormesis relaunch in May 2025, the business added new subscribers every month — but cancellations have outpaced new starts 10 out of 13 months. January 2026 was the one strong positive month (+1,787 net), driven by the $39 first-month promo. The churn floor appears structural: cancellations are running 5,000–5,800/month regardless of acquisition volume.

The Hormesis relaunch touched every part of the product experience (new formulas, packaging, names). The email account reflects that transition — 38 live flows plus a layer of legacy, draining, and draft flows that haven't been cleaned up. Three simultaneous refill transition variants are still running in parallel.

03

Biggest Gaps

  • 01
    Winback is essentially not functioning
    45,668 churned subscribers received winback emails in the last 12 months. The conversion rate is 0.21% — roughly 96 recoveries. At $79/month subscription value, each winback is worth $900+ in annualized revenue. The 48% open rate proves the churned audience is still reachable — the content and offer are failing to convert them. A single percentage point of improvement on 45K recipients would add ~450 re-subscribers annually.
    Urgent
  • 02
    No dunning flow — subscription payment failures are not being captured
    The Subscription Payment Failed metric shows zero events in the account. For a subscription-first business doing ~20K recurring orders per month, some percentage of those fail — credit card expirations, declines, bank blocks are universal. Either the metric is misconfigured (possible) or there is genuinely no recovery sequence for failed payments. This is a direct revenue leak.
    Urgent
  • 03
    Onboarding clicks are critically low
    M1 Onboarding has a 57.5% open rate but only 1.28% click rate across 80.9K recipients and 25 messages. The NYNM Mindshift flow, running in parallel, achieves 6.81% click on similar content. The opens are healthy — the audience is engaged. The M1 content is either not driving clear action, has weak CTAs, or isn't differentiated enough from the noise. There's a meaningful gap between what customers are reading and what they're doing.
    High impact
  • 04
    D30+ Engaged lead audience is unconverted
    120.9K leads with a 64.3% open rate — the highest open rate of any lead flow. These are 30-day engaged non-purchasers who are still actively opening emails. Conversion rate: 0.28%. The audience wants to engage. The content isn't closing. This is one of the highest-leverage optimization targets in the account.
    High leverage
  • 05
    SMS is absent from post-purchase lifecycle
    SMS is 100% promotional blast in this account — it has never been used in a lifecycle capacity for customers. No SMS in onboarding, refill reminders, or winback. Given that the SMS Welcome Flow converts at 5.7% and the refill reminder email converts at 23%, an SMS refill reminder — even at half the email rate — would be a meaningful revenue add. The infrastructure exists. The channel just isn't being used where it matters most.
    Channel gap
  • 06
    Campaign click rates are far below benchmark
    Email campaign click rates are running 0.2–0.8% against a 2–3% industry benchmark. The open rates are healthy (36–57%) — the audience is seeing the campaigns. Something in the content or offer architecture isn't compelling enough action. The $39 promo campaigns generated high volume but very low engagement per click.
    Revenue left on table
  • 07
    Sunset flow is likely a negative ROI send
    479,676 emails sent to disengaged leads, $0.005 RPR, 25 conversions, $2.4K revenue. Even at the lowest Klaviyo send cost, the math almost certainly doesn't work. A 31.7% open rate on this audience means significant subscriber activity — this list isn't as dead as a sunset flow implies. It should either be re-engaged with a real offer or suppressed more aggressively to protect deliverability.
    List hygiene risk
  • 08
    Devotion Cold Outreach has a 5.6% hard bounce rate
    Hard bounce rates above 2% are considered high-risk territory for email deliverability. At 5.6%, the Devotion Cold Outreach list is a material threat to the domain reputation that all other flows — including the $6.4M Refill flow — depend on. This needs to be cleaned immediately regardless of the program's other outcomes.
    Domain risk
  • 09
    No OTP → subscription conversion path
    There are active OTP segments (Recent, At Risk, Churned) and meaningful OTP volume, but no dedicated flow converting one-time purchasers to subscribers. OTP customers have already paid full price ($129) and proven intent — they're the highest-qualified audience for subscription conversion. There's no systematic mechanism to close the gap.
    Revenue gap
  • 10
    No systematic Stasis cross-sell
    Stasis is being cross-sold as a gifting tactic (Stasis Night included with new Thesis orders) but there's no dedicated cross-sell flow for existing Thesis subscribers. Stasis has a higher AOV ($95) and a specific audience (stimulant users) that likely has meaningful overlap with the Thesis subscriber base. The Stasis Purchases L30 segment was just created March 5 — a signal that this is being thought about, but it's not in the flow layer yet.
    Revenue opportunity

04

Strategic Opportunities

These are the moves with the clearest upside given what the data shows.

Winback rebuild

The 48% open rate proves churned customers are reachable. The current offer isn't working. A rebuild should test a real reactivation offer (formula swap, 60-day trial, price bridge), a shorter sequence with higher urgency, and an SMS component. Even tripling the 0.21% conversion rate adds ~300 re-subscribers per month.

Dunning sequence

Subscription payment failures are entirely unrecovered. The first step is confirming whether the Payment Failed metric is misconfigured or genuinely zero. If misconfigured, fix it. Then build a 3-touch sequence: immediate payment failure notification → retry reminder → last-chance with card update link. Standard dunning recovery rates are 5–15% of failures.

D30+ Engaged conversion

120K leads with 64% open rate and only 0.28% conversion. This audience is signaling interest every time they open. The existing content isn't converting them. Test a time-limited offer, a sharper product comparison, or a direct social proof message targeting this specific audience. The conversion opportunity per percentage point is significant.

SMS in lifecycle

SMS Welcome Flow converts at 5.7%. The refill reminder email converts at 23%. An SMS refill reminder — even at a fraction of that rate — would add direct revenue with minimal production cost. Start with a single SMS touchpoint at day 20 (before the refill window) for subscribed customers who have SMS consent.

OTP → subscription conversion

OTP customers at $129 already paid more than the first-month sub price ($59). Build a post-purchase flow targeting OTP purchasers at day 20–22 with a clear subscription offer: "You've been taking it for 3 weeks. Subscribe to make sure you don't run out." The existing OTP segments exist — there's no flow using them.

Referral program at scale

The Okendo Referral Opt In converts at 24.3% with $20.13 RPR — the highest RPR in the account. Only 632 people went through it in 12 months. The bottleneck is awareness. Promoting the referral program to active, satisfied subscribers (Super User cohort, Promoter reviewers) is a high-leverage activation with minimal cost.

05

30 / 60 / 90 Day View

Days 1–30
Account hygiene. Free up the metric ceiling — delete 7–10 dead/stale metrics. Archive draining and draft flows. Consolidate the 3 simultaneous Hormesis Transition refill variants.
Devotion list. Clean or suppress the Cold Outreach list immediately. 5.6% hard bounce is a deliverability risk to the entire domain.
Diagnose dunning. Confirm whether Subscription Payment Failed metric is misconfigured or genuinely zero. If misconfigured, fix the ReCharge ↔ Klaviyo event connection. If zero, build the trigger and a 3-touch dunning sequence.
Winback audit. Pull message-level performance on the existing winback flow. Understand what offer exists, what the sequence looks like, and what's failing. Build the brief for a rebuild.
Days 31–60
M1 Onboarding click rate. Audit all 25 messages in the M1 flow against the NYNM Mindshift benchmark. Identify messages with sub-1% click. Rewrite or replace with content that drives a single clear action.
D30+ Engaged offer. Test a direct, time-sensitive offer to the 120K engaged non-purchasers. This is the highest-leverage lead conversion opportunity in the account.
Winback rebuild live. New sequence with a real offer, a formula swap option, and an SMS arm for those who have consent.
OTP → subscription flow. Build and launch the day-20 post-OTP subscription conversion sequence using the existing OTP segments.
Days 61–90
SMS in lifecycle. Launch a single SMS refill reminder for subscribed customers who have SMS consent. Measure conversion lift vs. email-only cohort.
Referral program activation. Build a dedicated referral promotion sequence targeting the Super User list, Promoter reviewers, and tenured subscribers (6mo+).
Stasis cross-sell. Build a systematic cross-sell flow for Thesis subscribers using the new Stasis Purchases L30 segment as a model. Target: active Thesis subscribers at month 2+.
Campaign content test. Test a non-promotional campaign send to active subscribers — education or community content — to establish a baseline for what customer campaigns can do.
The account is performing well on the flows that are already working. The Refill flow alone is generating $6.4M attributed revenue on 23% conversion — that's not luck, that's a well-built retention mechanism. The opportunity is in the areas that are clearly not working: a winback with 0.21% conversion, a missing dunning sequence, an onboarding flow that isn't driving clicks, and a lead audience that's opening but not buying. These aren't complex problems. They're execution gaps with known solutions.
