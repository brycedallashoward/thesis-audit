Thesis Klaviyo Audit Fact Sheet Planning Planning II Audit

The Audit

Walkthrough for Dan Freed

Thesis + Stasis Klaviyo · March 2026

Portfolio context: Stasis now represents 49–53% of combined revenue (up from 35% eleven months ago) and has posted net subscriber growth in 8 of 11 months. Thesis is in structural subscriber decline — net loss in 10 of 13 months, with a fixed cancellation floor of ~5,000/mo that acquisition can’t outrun. The business is already responding by investing forward in Stasis, including the Kids launch. Neither brand has had a dedicated retention marketer. That’s the gap this engagement fills.
Tier 1 — Stasis Growth / Portfolio Foundation
1

Stasis Kids needs its own lifecycle — not a bolt-on to the adult journey

Stasis

The Kids launch is the brand’s active growth bet. It sold out in the test run — Dan already knows the demand is real. And the infrastructure is partially there: dedicated lead flow, post-purchase, backorder, waitlist, and refill reminders all exist. But the lifecycle around it hasn’t been rebuilt for the audience it serves.

Right now, a parent browsing Stasis Kids capsules gets the same browse abandonment messaging as an adult shopping for themselves. The abandoned checkout flow doesn’t distinguish Kids from adult products. Campaign sends aren’t segmented. This means a parent worried about giving their child a supplement — someone thinking about safety, school performance, and the guilt that comes with medicating — is receiving “3PM Wall” and “stimulant crash” creative designed for an adult managing their own ADHD.

These are fundamentally different buying psychologies. The adult ADHD customer is driven by productivity, focus, and stimulant management. The parent is driven by safety, clinical credibility, and other parents’ experiences. Every touchpoint that conflates these two audiences is a missed conversion and a trust signal going the wrong direction.

Recommendation

  • Dedicated Kids browse abandonment with parent-specific messaging: safety data, clinical backing, testimonials from other parents
  • Separate checkout flow for Kids products — urgency and value props need to speak to parental concerns, not adult self-optimization
  • Kids-specific post-purchase journey with parent-oriented expectation-setting (“what to look for in the first week,” dosing guidance, when to adjust)
  • Campaign segmentation: parents should never receive adult ADHD creative. Build the segment, enforce the split.
2

Post-purchase needs to become a conversation — not a content dump

Both

Both brands already have “what to expect” content on their product pages — timelines broken into hours, days, weeks, and months. It’s good content. But it sits on a PDP that the customer will never revisit after purchasing. None of it is delivered programmatically after the sale, and there’s no mechanism on either brand for the customer to tell you how it’s going.

This is where churn starts. A customer takes their first dose, isn’t sure what to expect, doesn’t feel anything dramatic on day one, and starts wondering if it’s working. By day 14, doubt has set in. By month two, they cancel. The brand had content that could have addressed every one of those moments — but it was trapped on a page the customer already left.

1.3% M1 onboarding click rate
6.8% Mindshift series click rate
5x Improvement with right content

Email alone isn’t working for this. Thesis M1 onboarding gets strong opens (57%) but critically low clicks (1.3%). The Mindshift series — which delivers education-first content about habit formation — hits 6.8% click to 37K recipients. The difference isn’t channel, it’s content approach. SMS adds a dimension email can’t: it invites a reply. It creates a feedback loop where the brand learns what’s happening, not just what it’s sending.

Recommendation

  • SMS milestone series anchored to the product experience: day 3 (“how are you feeling?”), week 1 (expectation-setting), week 2 (habit reinforcement), month 1 (assessment)
  • Feedback capture at each stage — this creates signal for retention intervention. Negative response at week 1 triggers a support check-in, not another marketing email.
  • Use the PDP “what to expect” content as raw material — it already exists, it just needs to be activated in the right channel at the right time
  • Close the loop: positive feedback at month 1 triggers a review request. Negative feedback triggers a conversation. Either way, you learn something.
3

The open-to-click gap is a portfolio-wide creative problem

Both

Across both brands, the pattern is the same: strong open rates, critically weak click rates. Thesis campaigns hit 36–57% opens with 0.2–0.8% clicks. Stasis shows the same shape. Thesis M1 onboarding — 25 messages to 81K recipients — gets 57% opens and 1.3% clicks. DTC email benchmark for click rate is 2–3%. Both brands are running at roughly 25% of that.

0.2–0.8% Campaign click rates
2–3% DTC benchmark
6.8% Mindshift (proof case)

The subject lines are working — people are opening. The creative inside the email is failing them. This isn’t a deliverability problem or a list quality problem. It’s a content and CTA problem, and it compounds across every flow and campaign on both accounts.

The proof that this is solvable already exists in the account. The Mindshift series (flow Y6nDb2) sends education-first content about the 30-day habit reset to the same Thesis audience and drives a 6.8% click rate — 5x the M1 onboarding rate. Same people, same channel, radically different result. When the content leads with value instead of promotion, clicks follow.

On the Stasis side, campaigns use condition-specific angles that resonate (“3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” “Social Battery”) — but none of these appear in the checkout or onboarding flows. The best-performing campaign creative hasn’t been translated into the lifecycle.

Recommendation

  • Audit the highest-volume flows and campaigns on both brands for CTA clarity and value proposition
  • Apply the Mindshift content model: education-first, clear “what you’ll learn/get” framing, value before ask
  • Stasis flows need the campaign angles: bring “3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” and condition-specific messaging into checkout, onboarding, and retention flows
  • The math: lifting click rates from 0.5% to 2% is a 4x improvement on every send across both brands. This is the single highest-leverage creative fix in the portfolio.

Stasis cancel reasons are invisible — retention messaging is flying blind

Stasis

Stasis is growing. 8 of 11 months net positive, with a massive Jan–Feb 2026 acquisition spike adding ~7,000 net subscribers in two months. But underneath the growth, there’s a persistent cancel floor: ~2,900–3,200 cancellations per month regardless of how many new subscribers come in. The cancel volume doesn’t spike with acquisition — it sits at its own fixed level, which means there’s a structural retention issue independent of growth.

The problem is that we can’t see why people are canceling. ReCharge cancellation events sync into Klaviyo with empty event properties — no cancel reason, no product context, nothing actionable. The cancel reason data lives exclusively in ReCharge’s native reporting. Until we can access it, every save flow and winback sequence is generic. A customer who cancelled because the price was too high gets the same message as one who had side effects or one who just forgot to pause instead of cancel.

This is a solvable data problem, and solving it unlocks the entire retention layer. Once we know reasons, we can build flows that actually address the objection: price sensitivity gets a downgrade or pause offer, product-fit issues get a SKU swap suggestion, “didn’t feel a difference” gets expectation-setting content timed to the product’s onset curve. And the trends feed forward — if the top cancel reason is “didn’t feel anything,” that tells us onboarding needs to set expectations earlier.

Recommendation

  • Get cancel reason data from ReCharge — direct access or an export from the team. This is the prerequisite for everything below.
  • Build reason-specific save flows: price → downgrade/pause offer; product-fit → SKU swap; “didn’t work” → timeline-setting content; accidental cancel → one-click reactivation
  • Reason-specific winback sequences: a customer who left over price needs a different re-engagement strategy than one who experienced side effects
  • Feed trends back into onboarding: if the top cancel reason maps to a solvable education gap, address it before the customer reaches the cancellation moment
5

Winback needs to be a conversation, not a broadcast

Both

Between Thesis and Stasis, there are roughly 165,000 churned subscribers who are still opening emails. That’s not a dead audience — it’s a massive, reachable group of people who already know the brand, already tried the product, and are still paying attention. The problem is what they’re getting.

~165K Churned subs still opening
48–57% Winback open rates
0.21–0.25% Winback conversion

Thesis winback converts at 0.21% across 46K recipients. Stasis winback converts at 0.25% across 118K. Both flows have open rates between 48% and 57% — these people are reading, they’re just not acting. The messaging is generic: “we miss you, come back.” Nobody’s asking them why they left. Nobody’s giving them a reason to respond. It’s a broadcast, not a conversation.

The opportunity is to turn winback into a feedback mechanism. Instead of sending a discount and hoping, create a moment where the customer tells you what happened. SMS at the 30-day cancel mark: “We’re all ears. Got questions, feedback, or concerns you want addressed? Reply to this message or click below.” In email, interactive modules: “How you felt on Stasis vs. without Stasis” with reaction CTAs. If they confirm they felt the benefit, they’re a warm winback candidate with a different offer strategy. If they didn’t, the product wasn’t for them — stop pushing, save the relationship for cross-sell.

Recommendation

  • SMS feedback layer at 30-day cancel mark — invite a response, not a purchase
  • Interactive email modules that let the customer self-segment: “felt the benefit” vs. “didn’t notice a difference”
  • Branch on response: warm candidates get a tailored re-engagement path; cold candidates get left alone or redirected to a different product
  • This creates signal, not just sends. Differentiate winback by what the customer tells you, not just by cancellation date.

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family — profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis. Messaging should feel like “we’re here for your whole family” — not separate product silos.

Combined portal — holistic digital experience across brands

Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family’s supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the “what to expect” content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM — Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks “How satisfied are you with your stimulants?” — gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There’s likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience — an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface.

Stimulant story discount — unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. Intentionally unstructured — free-text or voice — to capture authentic stories and language. Parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey.

Dynamic question series — progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails — a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization.
