Thesis Klaviyo Audit Fact Sheet Planning Planning II Audit

The Audit

Walkthrough for Dan Freed

Thesis + Stasis Klaviyo · March 2026

Portfolio context: Stasis now represents 49–53% of combined revenue (up from 35% eleven months ago) and has posted net subscriber growth in 8 of 11 months. Thesis is in structural subscriber decline. The business is investing forward in Stasis — including the Kids launch — and both brands have been operating without a dedicated retention marketer. The recommendations below reflect where Stasis should be built out and where Thesis revenue should be protected.
Tier 1 — Stasis Growth / Portfolio Foundation
1

Stasis Kids needs its own lifecycle — not a bolt-on to the adult journey

Kids launch has dedicated flows but browse abandonment, checkout, and campaigns don’t differentiate. Parent psychology ≠ adult ADHD customer.
Stasis

What we found

  • Kids launch infrastructure exists: dedicated lead flow, post-purchase, backorder, waitlist, refill reminders
  • But browse abandonment sends generic adult messaging to parents browsing Kids capsules
  • Abandoned checkout doesn’t distinguish Kids vs. adult products
  • Parent buying psychology (safety, school performance, guilt about medicating) is fundamentally different from adult ADHD customer (productivity, focus, stimulant management)

Why this is #1

This is the brand’s active growth bet. Dan already knows the demand is there — it sold out in the test run. The gap is that the lifecycle messaging doesn’t match the audience. A parent researching ADHD supplements for their child should not receive the same “3PM Wall” and “stimulant crash” messaging designed for adults.

Recommendation

  • Dedicated Kids browse abandonment with parent-specific messaging (safety, clinical backing, other parents’ experience)
  • Separate checkout flow for Kids products
  • Kids-specific post-purchase journey with parent-oriented expectation-setting
  • Campaign segmentation: parents should never receive adult ADHD creative
2

Post-purchase series for both brands — SMS feedback loop and guided “what to expect” journey

PDPs already have hours/days/weeks/months content. Turn it into programmatic guidance via SMS with feedback collection at each milestone.
Both

What we found

  • Both brands have “what to expect” content on PDPs (hours, days, weeks, months timelines)
  • This content exists but isn’t being delivered programmatically post-purchase
  • No SMS feedback layer exists on either brand — no mechanism to hear from customers during the critical adoption window
  • Thesis M1 onboarding gets 57% opens but 1.3% clicks — email alone isn’t creating engagement
  • Thesis Mindshift series (flow Y6nDb2) hits 6.81% click — 5x better — proving education content works when it’s the right format

Why this is #2

Post-purchase is where churn starts. Both brands are sending emails but not creating a conversation. SMS feedback at key milestones (day 3, week 1, week 2, month 1) gives the brand real signal on whether the product is working — and gives the customer a sense that someone’s listening. The content already exists on the PDPs. This is about activating it in the right channel at the right time, then layering in feedback collection to create a closed loop.

Recommendation

  • Build SMS milestone series: day 3 (“how are you feeling?”), week 1 (expectation-setting), week 2 (habit reinforcement), month 1 (assessment)
  • Capture feedback at each stage — this creates signal for retention intervention
  • Use the PDP “what to expect” content as the raw material for the series
  • Close the loop: negative feedback triggers support outreach, positive feedback triggers review request

Open-to-click gap is a portfolio-wide creative problem

Both brands: strong opens (36–57%), weak clicks (0.2–0.8%). Benchmark is 2–3%. Affects every send across both accounts.
Both

What we found

  • Thesis campaigns: 36–57% opens, 0.2–0.8% clicks
  • Stasis campaigns: similar pattern — strong opens, low click-through
  • Thesis M1 onboarding: 57% opens, 1.28% clicks (but Mindshift series hits 6.81% — 5x)
  • Stasis abandoned checkout: generic cart reminder without ADHD-specific value props
  • Click rates are ~25% of DTC email benchmark across both brands

The proof case

The Mindshift series (flow Y6nDb2) proves this is solvable. Same audience, same channel — but education-first content with a clear value proposition drives 5x the click rate. When the content is right, clicks follow. The gap isn’t deliverability or list quality — it’s creative and content strategy.

Recommendation

  • Audit the highest-volume flows and campaigns on both brands for CTA clarity and value proposition
  • Apply the Mindshift content model: education-first, clear “what you’ll learn/get” framing
  • On Stasis: bring the condition-specific campaign angles (“3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” “Social Battery”) into flows where they’re currently absent
  • Fixing click rates from 0.5% to 2% is a 4x improvement on every send across both brands

Stasis cancel reasons are invisible — retention messaging is flying blind

ReCharge cancellation events sync to Klaviyo with empty properties. Cancel reason data lives in ReCharge native reporting only. No reason-specific save or winback messaging exists.
Stasis

What we found

  • Stasis cancels ~2,900–3,200/mo against ~2,900–7,000 starts — 8 of 11 months net positive, but the cancel floor is persistent
  • ReCharge cancellation events coming into Klaviyo have empty event properties — no cancel reason, no product context
  • Cancel reason data lives exclusively in ReCharge’s native reporting — we need direct ReCharge access or a cancel reason export
  • Without reasons, any winback or cancellation-prevention messaging is generic — no ability to address price sensitivity differently from product dissatisfaction or shipping issues

The opportunity

  • Get cancel reason data from ReCharge (direct access or export)
  • Build reason-specific cancellation save flows: price objection gets a downgrade/pause offer, product-fit issues get SKU swap suggestions, “didn’t work” gets timeline-setting content
  • Reason-specific winback sequences: a customer who cancelled for price needs a different message than one who had side effects
  • Feed cancel reason trends back into onboarding — if “didn’t feel a difference” is top reason, front-load expectation-setting content

Winback needs to be a conversation, not a broadcast

Thesis: 48% opens, 0.21% conversion on 46K. Stasis: 48–57% opens, 0.25% on 118K. Both audiences are reachable — nobody’s asking them why they left.
Both

What we found

  • Thesis: 59,319 churned subscribers still engaged in last 90 days. Current winback converts 0.21%.
  • Stasis: 118K+ recipients through winback, $14K total revenue, 0.25% conversion
  • Both flows have strong open rates (48–57%) — these people are reading
  • Combined: ~165K churned subscribers still paying attention, getting generic messaging

Recommendation

  • Add an SMS feedback layer at the 30-day cancel mark: “We’re all ears. Got questions, feedback, or concerns you want addressed? Reply to this message or click below.”
  • Use interactive modules in email: “How you felt on Stasis vs. without Stasis” with CTAs to let the customer react
  • If they confirm they felt the benefit: warm winback candidate, different offer strategy
  • If they didn’t: product wasn’t for them, stop pushing — save the relationship for cross-sell
  • This creates signal, not just sends. Differentiate winback by what the customer tells you, not just by cancellation date.

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family — profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis (stimulant users). Messaging should feel like “we’re here for your whole family” — not separate product silos.

Combined portal — holistic digital experience across brands

Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family’s supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the “what to expect” content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM — Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks “How satisfied are you with your stimulants?” — gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There’s likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience — an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface. The “what to expect” content on PDPs is the seed; the feedback loop is the differentiator.

Stimulant story discount — unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. This is intentionally unstructured — free-text or voice — to capture authentic stories and language. We can then parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey. The discount drives participation; the data drives personalization.

Dynamic question series — progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails — a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization.
