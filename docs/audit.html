Tier 1 — Stasis Growth / Portfolio Foundation
1

Stasis Kids is getting adult messaging

Stasis

Kids sold out in the test run. The demand is proven and the infrastructure is partially built — dedicated lead flow, post-purchase, backorder, waitlist, refill reminders all exist. What hasn’t been built is a lifecycle that speaks to the actual buyer.

A parent browsing Stasis Kids right now gets the same browse abandonment emails as an adult shopping for themselves. Checkout doesn’t split Kids from adult. Campaigns aren’t segmented. So a parent thinking about safety, school performance, and whether this is the right call for their kid is getting “3PM Wall” and “stimulant crash” copy written for an adult managing their own ADHD.

The adult customer cares about productivity and focus. The parent cares about safety, clinical evidence, and what other parents have experienced. These are different conversations, and right now every touchpoint is having the wrong one with the Kids audience.

Recommendation

  • Kids browse abandonment — parent messaging: safety, clinical backing, other parents’ experiences
  • Kids checkout flow — different urgency, different value props. Parental concerns, not self-optimization.
  • Kids post-purchase journey — what to look for in the first week, dosing, when to adjust
  • Segment enforcement — parents should never receive adult ADHD creative
2

Nobody hears from the customer after they buy

Both

Both brands have “what to expect” content on their product pages — hours, days, weeks, months timelines. Good content. But it’s sitting on a page the customer will never revisit after purchasing. None of it gets delivered after the sale, and neither brand has any mechanism for the customer to tell you how it’s going.

Someone takes their first dose, doesn’t feel anything dramatic on day one, and starts wondering if it works. By day 14, doubt sets in. By month two, they cancel. The content that could have addressed every one of those moments existed — it just never reached them.

1.3% M1 onboarding click rate
6.8% Mindshift series click rate
5x Improvement with right content

Email alone isn’t getting it done here. M1 onboarding gets 57% opens but 1.3% clicks. The Mindshift series — education content about habit formation — hits 6.8% click to the same audience. The difference is content approach, not channel. But SMS adds something email can’t: it invites a reply. You actually hear back from the customer. That’s the missing piece.

Recommendation

  • SMS milestone series: day 3 (“how are you feeling?”), week 1 (set expectations), week 2 (reinforce the habit), month 1 (check in)
  • Capture feedback at each stage. Negative response at week 1 triggers a support check-in, not another marketing email.
  • The PDP content is the raw material — it’s already written, just not being delivered after the sale
  • Close the loop: good feedback at month 1 → review request. Bad feedback → conversation. Either way, you learn something.
3

People are opening everything and clicking nothing

Both

Same pattern on both brands: strong opens, almost no clicks. Thesis campaigns hit 36–57% opens with 0.2–0.8% clicks. Stasis looks the same. M1 onboarding — 25 messages to 81K people — gets 57% opens and 1.3% clicks. DTC benchmark is 2–3%. Both brands are at about a quarter of that.

0.2–0.8% Campaign click rates
2–3% DTC benchmark
6.8% Mindshift (proof case)

Subject lines work. People open. What’s inside the email doesn’t give them a reason to click. This isn’t deliverability, it’s not list quality — it’s the content and CTAs, and it compounds across every send on both accounts.

The proof that it’s fixable is already in the account. The Mindshift series (flow Y6nDb2) — education content about the 30-day habit reset — drives 6.8% click to the same Thesis audience. Same people, same channel, 5x the result. When the content leads with something worth clicking for, they click.

Stasis campaigns already use condition-specific angles that work (“3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” “Social Battery”). None of that appears in the checkout or onboarding flows. The best campaign creative hasn’t made it into the lifecycle yet.

Recommendation

  • Audit highest-volume flows and campaigns on both brands for CTA clarity and value prop
  • Model on Mindshift: lead with education, make the click worth something, value before ask
  • Get Stasis campaign angles into the flows: “3PM Wall,” “Stimulant crash,” condition-specific messaging belongs in checkout, onboarding, and retention — not just campaigns
  • The math: 0.5% → 2% is a 4x lift on every send across both brands

We don’t know why Stasis customers cancel

Stasis

Stasis is growing — 8 of 11 months net positive, with Jan–Feb 2026 adding ~7,000 net subscribers. But there’s a cancel floor that doesn’t move: ~2,900–3,200 cancellations per month regardless of how many new subscribers come in. Acquisition goes up, cancels stay flat at their own level. That’s a retention problem running on its own track.

We can’t see why. ReCharge cancellation events sync into Klaviyo with empty properties — no cancel reason, no product context. That data lives in ReCharge’s native reporting only. So right now, every save flow and winback sends the same message whether someone cancelled over price, side effects, or because they meant to pause.

This is a data problem with a clear fix. Once we have the reasons, we can build flows that actually respond: price → downgrade or pause offer, product-fit → SKU swap, “didn’t feel a difference” → expectation-setting content. And the trends feed forward — if the top reason is “didn’t feel anything,” onboarding needs to set expectations earlier.

Recommendation

  • Get cancel reason data from ReCharge — direct access or an export from the team. This is the prerequisite for everything below.
  • Build reason-specific save flows: price → downgrade/pause offer; product-fit → SKU swap; “didn’t work” → timeline-setting content; accidental cancel → one-click reactivation
  • Reason-specific winback sequences: a customer who left over price needs a different re-engagement strategy than one who experienced side effects
  • Feed trends back into onboarding: if the top cancel reason maps to a solvable education gap, address it before the customer reaches the cancellation moment
5

165K churned subscribers are still reading — and getting nothing useful

Both

Combined across both brands, about 165,000 cancelled subscribers are still opening emails. They know the brand, they tried the product, they’re still paying attention. The problem is what they’re getting back.

~165K Churned subs still opening
48–57% Winback open rates
0.21–0.25% Winback conversion

Thesis winback: 0.21% conversion across 46K. Stasis: 0.25% across 118K. Open rates are 48–57% on both — they’re reading, they’re just not acting. The messaging is “we miss you, come back” with no ask, no feedback capture, no reason to respond.

Flip it. Instead of broadcasting a discount and hoping, create a moment where the customer tells you what happened. SMS at 30 days post-cancel: “Got questions, feedback, or concerns? Reply to this message.” In email, interactive modules: “How you felt on Stasis vs. without it” — click to react. If they felt the benefit, they’re a warm winback candidate. If they didn’t, stop pushing and save the relationship for something else.

Recommendation

  • SMS at 30-day cancel mark — invite a response, not a purchase
  • Interactive email modules that let the customer self-sort: felt the benefit vs. didn’t notice a difference
  • Branch on what they tell you: warm candidates get a re-engagement path, cold candidates get left alone or pointed somewhere else
  • Winback should create signal, not just volume. Segment by what the customer says, not just how long ago they cancelled.

  • Launch Surprise & Delight as a retention signal — no conversion ask, just relationship-building

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family — profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis. Messaging should feel like “we’re here for your whole family” — not separate product silos.

Combined portal — holistic digital experience across brands

Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family’s supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the “what to expect” content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM — Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks “How satisfied are you with your stimulants?” — gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There’s likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience — an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface.

Stimulant story discount — unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. Intentionally unstructured — free-text or voice — to capture authentic stories and language. Parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey.

Dynamic question series — progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails — a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization.
