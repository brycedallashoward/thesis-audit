Tier 1 — Structural / Highest Impact
1

Structural churn is the core problem — everything else is downstream

30–38% monthly churn rate. Net subscriber loss in 10 of 13 months. Revenue is a lagging indicator.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Monthly cancellations are a fixed floor: 4,859–5,810/mo regardless of how many new subs come in
  • Revenue is stable ($1.9M–$2.3M/mo) only because the existing subscriber base hasn't fully decayed yet
  • Acquisition campaigns (Aug Labor Day, Nov BFCM, Jan $39 promo) temporarily spike new subs but never move the churn floor
  • To grow by 1,000 subs/month requires acquiring ~6,000 new subs to offset ~5,000 cancels

Why this is #1

Every other opportunity on this list is either a symptom of this problem or a partial solution to it. The business is running acquisition hard enough to mask the decline, but the math is unsustainable. This frames the entire audit narrative.

What to show

  • 13-month subscriber trend chart: new subs vs. cancels vs. net
  • Revenue stability vs. subscriber decline overlay — the lag
  • Implied retention curve (65–70% after M1, 45–50% after M2, 30–35% after M3)
2

M1 onboarding has attention but no action

57% opens, 1.3% clicks across 25 messages. Mindshift series proves education content works better (6.8% click).
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • 80,930 recipients through M1 onboarding in 12 months
  • Opens are strong (57.5%) — subscribers are reading
  • Clicks are critically low (1.28%) — nothing in those emails drives action
  • NYNM Mindshift series has 60.3% open and 6.81% click — 5x the click rate — proving education/mindset content resonates far better than standard onboarding

Why this is #2

Onboarding is the single biggest lever against Month 1–2 churn. These subscribers are paying attention and not being given a reason to engage. The Mindshift series shows the format that works — it's not a mystery, it's a content problem.

What to show

  • M1 open vs. click rate comparison with Mindshift series
  • Message-by-message click rate to find which emails are dead weight
  • Recommendation: restructure M1 around education/value content, not transactional messaging
3

Campaign creative converts attention but not clicks

Open rates 36–57%. Click rates 0.2–0.8% (benchmark 2–3%). Subject lines work, creative doesn't.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Consistent pattern across all campaign types: strong opens, extremely weak clicks
  • Click rates are roughly 25% of benchmark for DTC email
  • This is a design/CTA/content problem, not an audience quality problem (opens prove the audience is there)

Why this is #3

This affects every campaign send. The audience is opening — the creative is failing them. Fixing click rates from 0.5% to 2% is a 4x improvement on every campaign sent, across leads, churned, and active audiences.

What to show

  • Open vs. click gap across campaign types
  • Benchmark comparison
  • Hypothesis: CTA placement, email design, or copy relevance — needs creative audit
4

48K warm churned subscribers are reachable but not being re-engaged effectively

59K churned engaged in 90d, only 11K are "recent." 48K are still opening emails but have no dedicated re-engagement path.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • 59,319 churned subscribers are still engaged (opened/clicked in last 90 days)
  • Only 11,252 are "recent" cancels — the other 48K cancelled long ago but are still paying attention
  • Current winback flow: 48% open rate but 0.21% conversion (99.8% failure rate) on 45,668 recipients
  • The audience is warm. The offer and messaging aren't working.

Why this is #4

This is 48K people who already know the product, already cancelled, and are still opening emails. They're reachable. The current winback content/offer isn't giving them a reason to come back. Even recovering 2–3% of this pool would be meaningful revenue.

What to show

  • Churned engaged segment size vs. current winback conversion
  • Winback flow open rate (48%) vs. conversion (0.21%) gap
  • Revenue math: even 500 reactivations/year at $79/mo = significant
Tier 2 — High Impact / Clear Signal
5

SMS channel is high-performing but damaged and abandoned

Best lead acquisition RPR ($6.23) but list shrank 18% in 25 days after the $39 blast. Zero sends in recent 30d.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • SMS Welcome Flow: $6.23 RPR, 5.69% conversion — best lead acquisition channel by far
  • $39 promo SMS blast (Jan 6, 2026): 5.15% unsub rate = ~2,600 permanent list losses in one send
  • SMS list shrank from 50.7K to 41.4K over 25 days
  • Zero SMS in any post-purchase flow (onboarding, refill, winback)
  • SMS appears to have stopped entirely in recent weeks

Why this is #5

The channel works when used correctly (welcome flow proves it). It was damaged by aggressive promotional blasting. The question for Dan is whether SMS is worth rebuilding or if the list erosion is too far gone.

What to show

  • SMS Welcome Flow performance vs. email lead flow
  • List decline timeline
  • Gap: SMS is 100% promotional, 0% lifecycle
6

Review completion rate declining — flow timing and incentive structure need rework

0.88% completion (down from 1.4%). Benchmark is 1.5–3%. ~20K requests/mo yielding only 130–155 reviews.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Completion rate nearly halved over 12 months (1.4% → 0.6–0.7%)
  • Average rating drifting: 4.59 → 4.45; 5-star share dropped 82% → 72%
  • Negative themes: efficacy expectations, GI issues, subscription friction, price, caffeine (Motivation)
  • Product transition visible: old blends declining, Clarity Refill dominant, Neuroprotection appearing

Why this is #6

Reviews are declining in both quantity and quality. The rating drift and negative themes may also be early signals of the product-market problems feeding churn. The review flow is a quick-win optimization, but the sentiment data is strategically valuable beyond just "get more reviews."

What to show

  • 12-month completion rate trend
  • Rating distribution shift
  • Negative sentiment themes — these tell the churn story from the customer's perspective
7

D30+ Engaged Leads: high attention, low conversion

120K recipients, 64% open rate, 0.28% conversion. This audience wants to engage — content isn't closing.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • 120,877 recipients in 12 months with a 64% open rate — one of the highest engagement signals in the account
  • 0.28% conversion and $0.25 RPR — extremely low given the open engagement
  • Same pattern as M1 onboarding and campaigns: attention without action

Why this is #7

This is a large, warm audience. The gap between 64% opens and 0.28% conversion is one of the clearest creative/offer problems in the account. Fixing the content here has a much larger addressable audience than most other optimizations.

What to show

  • Open-to-conversion gap
  • What content/offers these leads are currently receiving
  • Comparison to education campaign RPR ($0.01) — pattern of education without conversion mechanism
8

Referral program has highest RPR in the account but near-zero volume

Okendo Referral Opt In: $20.13 RPR on only 632 recipients. Awareness is the bottleneck.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Referral opt-in flow: $20.13 RPR, 24.3% conversion — the single highest-performing flow by revenue per recipient
  • Review incentive flows also strong: $4.63–$4.79 RPR
  • But volume is tiny — 632 and 2,748 recipients respectively

Why this is #8

The flow works. The problem is nobody knows about it or is being driven into it. If referral participation could be scaled 5–10x, it becomes a meaningful acquisition channel at very low cost. This is about distribution, not flow optimization.

What to show

  • RPR comparison: referral vs. all other flows
  • Volume gap
  • Where referral could be surfaced (post-purchase, milestone, onboarding)
9

No product-specific onboarding — all formulas get the same messaging

Clarity, Motivation, Stress Reset, and Neuroprotection have different benefit stories. M1/M2 flows don't differentiate.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • M1 and M2 onboarding flows send the same content regardless of which formula the customer subscribed to
  • Each formula has a different use case, benefit timeline, and common objection profile
  • Clarity migration flow (RhVArK) is product-specific and outperforms: 51.6% open, 38.2% conversion — showing that product-aware messaging works

Why this is #9

Generic onboarding for a product with 4+ distinct formulas is a missed opportunity. The Clarity migration flow proves product-specific messaging converts at a much higher rate. This compounds with the M1 click-rate problem (#2).

What to show

  • Current M1 flow: one path for all products
  • Clarity migration flow performance as proof of concept
  • Negative review themes by product (e.g., caffeine/Motivation, GI/certain blends) as input for product-specific education
