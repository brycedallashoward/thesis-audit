Portfolio context: Stasis now represents 49–53% of combined revenue (up from 35% eleven months ago) and has posted net subscriber growth in 8 of 11 months. Thesis is in structural subscriber decline. The business is investing forward in Stasis — including the Kids launch — and both brands are deeply underworked on retention. Recommendations are ordered by where we see the most potential across the portfolio, not just where the biggest problems are.
Tier 1 — Structural / Highest Impact
1

Stasis Kids needs its own lifecycle — not a bolt-on to the adult journey

Kids launch has dedicated flows but browse abandonment, checkout, and campaigns don't differentiate. Parent psychology ≠ adult ADHD customer.
Stasis ▶

What we found

  • Kids launch infrastructure exists: dedicated lead flow, post-purchase, backorder, waitlist, refill reminders
  • But browse abandonment sends generic adult messaging to parents browsing Kids capsules
  • Abandoned checkout doesn't distinguish Kids vs. adult products
  • Kids announcement campaign performed well ($15.6K from 53K sends, $0.29 RPR) — proving there's demand
  • Parent buying psychology (safety, school performance, guilt about medicating) is fundamentally different from adult ADHD customer (productivity, focus, stimulant management)

Why this is #1

This is the brand's active growth bet. The Kids product is new, the demand signal is strong, and the messaging gap is wide open. Getting this wrong means the highest-momentum product in the portfolio converts below its potential. A parent researching ADHD supplements for their child should not receive the same "3PM Wall" and "stimulant crash" messaging designed for adults.

What to show

  • Kids announcement campaign performance as proof of demand
  • Browse/checkout flow audit: where Kids visitors get adult messaging
  • Recommendation: dedicated Kids browse abandonment, checkout, and post-purchase paths
2

Open-to-click gap is a portfolio-wide creative problem

Both brands: strong opens (36–57%), weak clicks (0.2–0.8%). Benchmark is 2–3%. Affects every send across both accounts.
Both ▶

What we found

  • Thesis campaigns: 36–57% opens, 0.2–0.8% clicks
  • Stasis campaigns: similar pattern — strong opens, low click-through
  • Thesis M1 onboarding: 57% opens, 1.28% clicks (but Mindshift series hits 6.81% clicks — 5x)
  • Stasis abandoned checkout: generic cart reminder without ADHD-specific value props, social proof, or urgency
  • Click rates are ~25% of DTC email benchmark across both brands

Why this is #2

This is the single highest-leverage optimization because it compounds across every flow and campaign in both accounts. The audience is opening — the creative is failing them. Fixing click rates from 0.5% to 2% is a 4x improvement on every send. The Mindshift series proves that when content is right, clicks follow.

What to show

  • Open vs. click gap across both brands
  • Mindshift series as the proof case (education content → 5x click rate)
  • Stasis abandoned checkout as specific example: where ADHD-specific messaging and value props are missing
  • Recommendation: creative/CTA audit across both brands
3

Winback is broken on both brands — warm audiences, wrong content

Thesis: 48% opens, 0.21% conversion on 46K recipients. Stasis: 48–57% opens, 0.25% conversion on 118K recipients. Both audiences are reachable.
Both ▶

What we found

  • Thesis: 59,319 churned subscribers still engaged in last 90 days. Current winback converts 0.21%.
  • Stasis: 118K+ recipients through winback flow, $14K total revenue, 0.25% conversion
  • Both flows have strong open rates (48–57%) — these people are reading the emails
  • Combined pool: ~165K churned subscribers across both brands are still paying attention but the offer/messaging isn't giving them a reason to come back

Why this is #3

This is the largest addressable audience that already knows both products. 165K churned subscribers are still opening emails. Improving winback conversion from 0.2% to even 2% across both brands is thousands of reactivated subscribers per year. The audience quality is proven — it's purely a content and offer problem.

What to show

  • Side-by-side winback performance: Thesis vs. Stasis (same problem, both brands)
  • Open rate vs. conversion gap
  • Thesis warm pool: 48K non-recent churned still engaging
  • Recommendation: differentiated winback by cancellation reason and product, not one-size-fits-all
4

Thesis structural churn is the context — the business is already shifting investment toward Stasis

30–38% monthly churn. Net subscriber loss 10 of 13 months. Revenue is a lagging indicator. Stasis is growing; Thesis is not.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Thesis cancellations are a fixed floor: 4,859–5,810/mo regardless of acquisition volume
  • Revenue stable ($1.9M–$2.3M/mo) only because existing subscriber base hasn't fully decayed
  • Stasis: 8 of 11 months net positive subscribers, cumulative +9,253. Revenue share growing from 35% to 53%.
  • Campaign calendar went dark on Thesis after Jan 31. Stasis continued.
  • The business is already signaling where it's going

Why this is #4

This frames the audit narrative. Thesis churn is real and the math is unsustainable, but the business is already responding by investing in Stasis. The audit should acknowledge this reality — not pretend Thesis is the primary growth lever. Retention work on Thesis protects existing revenue; retention work on Stasis accelerates where the business is headed.

What to show

  • Side-by-side subscriber trajectories: Thesis declining, Stasis growing
  • Revenue share shift over 11 months
  • Campaign activity comparison: Thesis dark, Stasis active
  • Frame: protect Thesis revenue while building Stasis retention infrastructure

6

Stasis abandoned checkout doesn't sell — it just reminds

$2.16 RPR on 10.5K recipients. First email is a generic cart reminder without ADHD value props, social proof, or urgency.
Stasis ▶

What we found

  • Abandoned checkout converts at 1.9% — reasonable but well below potential
  • First email is a generic cart reminder: no ADHD-specific messaging, no value props, no social proof
  • Stasis's strongest campaign creative uses condition-specific angles ("3PM Wall," "Stimulant crash," "Social Battery") — none of this appears in the checkout flow
  • Second email timing is too late

Why this is #6

Checkout abandonment is highest-intent traffic. The ADHD messaging that works in campaigns ("relief from stimulant side effects," "freedom from the crash") is completely absent from the flow that catches people at decision point. This is a content problem with a clear template — the campaign creative already has the angles.

What to show

  • Current checkout flow content vs. top-performing campaign messaging
  • Gap: generic cart reminder vs. ADHD-specific conversion messaging
  • Kids vs. adult split missing
7

Thesis M1 onboarding has attention but no action

57% opens, 1.3% clicks across 25 messages. Mindshift series proves education content works better (6.8% click).
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • 80,930 recipients through M1 onboarding in 12 months
  • Opens are strong (57.5%) — subscribers are reading
  • Clicks are critically low (1.28%) — nothing in those emails drives action
  • NYNM Mindshift series: 60.3% open, 6.81% click — 5x the click rate — education/mindset content resonates far better than standard onboarding

Why this is #7

Onboarding is the single biggest lever against Month 1–2 churn on Thesis. These subscribers are paying attention and not being given a reason to engage. The Mindshift series shows the format that works.

What to show

  • M1 open vs. click rate comparison with Mindshift series
  • Message-by-message click rate to find which emails are dead weight
  • Recommendation: restructure M1 around education/value content
8

SMS is a proven channel being misused or abandoned on both brands

Thesis: best lead RPR ($6.23), list destroyed by $39 blast, now silent. Stasis: $2.58 RPR, 3.6% unsub on welcome, SMS abandon flows sitting in draft.
Both ▶

What we found

  • Thesis SMS Welcome: $6.23 RPR, 5.69% conversion — best lead channel. But $39 blast caused 5.15% unsub rate, list shrank 50.7K → 41.4K, and SMS appears stopped
  • Stasis SMS Welcome: $2.58 RPR, solid. But SMS list is small (~7K) and abandonment flows are in draft, not live
  • Neither brand uses SMS in lifecycle flows (onboarding, refill, winback) — 100% promotional on Thesis, barely used on Stasis

Why this is #8

SMS converts when used correctly on both brands. Thesis damaged its list through aggressive blasting; Stasis has built SMS flows but hasn't launched them. The channel is high-potential but currently either abandoned or underactivated.

What to show

  • SMS performance side-by-side: both brands prove it works
  • Thesis list decline timeline
  • Stasis draft SMS flows that should be live
9

Thesis review program declining — sentiment data tells the churn story

0.88% completion (down from 1.4%). Rating drifting 4.59 → 4.45. Negative themes mirror the reasons people cancel.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Completion rate nearly halved over 12 months (1.4% → 0.6–0.7%). Benchmark: 1.5–3%
  • Average rating drifting: 4.59 → 4.45; 5-star share dropped 82% → 72%
  • Negative themes: "doesn't work" expectations, GI issues, subscription friction, price, caffeine on Motivation
  • These themes map directly to why people churn

Why this is #9

The review flow is a quick-win optimization, but the real value is the sentiment data. The negative themes are a direct window into the churn problem (#4). When people write "doesn't work" in reviews, that's the same reason they cancel subscriptions.

What to show

  • 12-month completion rate trend
  • Rating distribution shift
  • Negative sentiment themes mapped to churn drivers

Referral program has highest RPR in Thesis but near-zero volume

Okendo Referral Opt In: $20.13 RPR on only 632 recipients. The flow works — awareness is the bottleneck.
Thesis ▶

What we found

  • Referral opt-in flow: $20.13 RPR, 24.3% conversion — highest-performing flow by RPR
  • Review incentive flows also strong: $4.63–$4.79 RPR

The flow works on both brands. The problem is nobody is being driven into it. If referral participation could be scaled 5–10x, it becomes a meaningful acquisition channel at very low cost.

What to show

  • RPR comparison: referral vs. all other flows
  • Volume gap
  • Where referral could be surfaced (post-purchase, milestone, onboarding)
