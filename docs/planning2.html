Winback needs to be a conversation, not a broadcast

Thesis: 48% opens, 0.21% conversion on 46K. Stasis: 48–57% opens, 0.25% on 118K. Both audiences are reachable — nobody's asking them why they left.
Both ▶

What we found

  • Thesis: 59,319 churned subscribers still engaged in last 90 days. Current winback converts 0.21%.
  • Stasis: 118K+ recipients through winback, $14K total revenue, 0.25% conversion
  • Both flows have strong open rates (48–57%) — these people are reading
  • Combined: ~165K churned subscribers still paying attention, getting generic messaging

The approach

  • Add an SMS feedback layer — at the 30-day cancel mark: "We're all ears. Got questions, feedback, or concerns you want addressed? Reply to this message or click below."
  • Use interactive modules in email: "How you felt on Stasis vs. without Stasis" with CTAs to let the customer react — did you feel this way? Yes or no?
  • If they confirm they felt the benefit: warm winback candidate, different offer strategy
  • If they didn't: product wasn't for them, stop pushing — save the relationship for something else
  • This creates signal, not just sends. Differentiate winback by what the customer tells you, not just by cancellation date.

Cross-brand cancel redirect

  • Neither brand’s cancel flow introduces the other brand. A Stasis canceller who was taking it alongside a stimulant still has the stimulant problem — Thesis’s value prop (get off stimulants entirely) is directly relevant.
  • A Thesis canceller citing focus or energy complaints could be a natural Stasis fit.
  • The cancel moment is when someone is most open to an alternative from the same company. Different mechanism than #7 (proactive cross-sell to active subs) — this is a redirect for people who are already leaving.

What to show

  • Side-by-side winback performance: both brands, same problem
  • Open rate vs. conversion gap
  • Proposal: conversational winback with feedback capture and interactive segmentation
  • Cross-brand cancel redirect as a winback variant

Future Big Bets

Stasis Family — profile-level family awareness and cross-sell

Introduce the concept of a Stasis family. For customers who have kids on Stasis Kids, profiles should reflect family context. This opens cross-sell in both directions: a Stasis Kids parent might be a candidate for adult Stasis or Thesis (stimulant users). Messaging should feel like "we're here for your whole family" — not separate product silos.

Combined portal — holistic digital experience across brands

Currently unclear what the portal experience is (if one exists). Long-term vision: a unified digital experience where a customer can manage their family's supplements, track their journey, and get personalized guidance. This is where the "what to expect" content, feedback loops, and product recommendations converge into one place.

Open the TAM — Stasis beyond stimulant users

The Stasis quiz immediately asks "How satisfied are you with your stimulants?" — gating on stimulant use from the first question. This excludes people overexposed to caffeine, managing ADHD without stimulants, or exploring non-pharmaceutical support. There's likely room to broaden the entry point and capture a wider audience.

Post-purchase as a digital experience

The SMS feedback journey (#2 above) could evolve into a richer digital experience — an interactive guided journey through hours, days, weeks, months of product use. Starts as SMS, could become its own surface. The “what to expect” content on PDPs is the seed; the feedback loop is the differentiator.

Stimulant story discount — unstructured data collection through incentive

Offer a special discount if a customer types or records their relationship with stimulants. This is intentionally unstructured — free-text or voice — to capture authentic stories and language. We can then parse the responses to build richer customer profiles, identify messaging themes that resonate, and customize content based on where someone actually is in their stimulant journey. The discount drives participation; the data drives personalization.

Dynamic question series — progressive profiling embedded in email

Include a small question toward the bottom of emails — a single, low-friction prompt with button-click responses. Once a customer answers one question, the next email includes the next question in the series. Over time this builds a rich profile without ever asking the customer to fill out a form. Each answer feeds segmentation and content personalization. The questions can cover stimulant history, goals, lifestyle factors, product experience — whatever helps us tailor the journey.

Community done right

Both Stasis and Thesis have modest community presences (under 3,000 followers/members each). Stasis emails are full of Instagram CTAs but the community itself hasn’t been built to support them. A concentrated community effort could turn this into a real pillar: peer support, product feedback, UGC for campaigns, and a retention lever that doesn’t depend on email or SMS.
